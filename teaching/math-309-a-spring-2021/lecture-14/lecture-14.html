Solving the PDE

Recall the heat equation with Dirichlet boundary conditions.

\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} \partial_tu(x,t)-c^2\partial_x^2u(x,t)=0&x\in[0,L],t>0\\ u(x,0)=f(x)&x\in[0,L]\\ u(0,t)=u(L,t)=0& t>0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}

Solutions to Eigenvalue Problem

  • \(X_k(x)=\sin\big(\frac{k\pi}{L}x\big)\)
  • \(T_k(t)=e^{-\big(\frac{ck\pi}{L}\big)^2t}\)
  • \(\lambda_k=-\big(\frac{k\pi}{L}\big)^2\)

For \(k=1,2,3,\cdots\)

Observations

  • \(\int_0^L X_j(x)X_k(x)dx=\begin{cases} 0 & j\neq k\\ \frac{L}{2} & j=k\end{cases}\)
  • \(\lambda_k\le 0\)

Specific Solutions

Let \(u_k(x,t)=T_k(t)X_k(x)\) Then \(u_k\) is a solution to

\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} \partial_tu(x,t)-c^2\partial_x^2u(x,t)=0&x\in[0,L],t>0\\ u(0,t)=u(L,t)=0& t>0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}

and \(u_k(x,0)=X_k(x)\)

Specific Solutions

Thus, \(u_k\) is the solution to

\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} \partial_tu(x,t)-c^2\partial_x^2u(x,t)=0&x\in[0,L],t>0\\ u(x,0)=\sin(k\pi x/L)&x\in[0,L]\\ u(0,t)=u(L,t)=0& t>0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}

Finite Linear Combinations

Let \(A_1,\cdots A_n\) be real numbers. Let \(u(x,t)=A_1u_1(x,t)+\cdots+A_n u_n(x,t)\). Then \(u\) is a solution to

\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} \partial_tu(x,t)-c^2\partial_x^2u(x,t)=0&x\in[0,L],t>0\\ u(0,t)=u(L,t)=0& t>0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}

and \(u(x,0)=A_1X_1(x)+A_2X_2(x)+\cdots+A_nX_n(x)\)

Finite Linear Combinations

Thus, \(u\) is the solution to

\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} \partial_tu(x,t)-c^2\partial_x^2u(x,t)=0&x\in[0,L],t>0\\ u(x,0)=A_1\sin(\pi x/L)+A_2\sin(2\pi x/L)+\cdots+A_n\sin(n\pi x/L)&x\in[0,L]\\ u(0,t)=u(L,t)=0& t>0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}

Example

Solve

\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} \partial_tu(x,t)-4\partial_x^2u(x,t)=0&x\in[0,3],t>0\\ u(x,0)=\sin(\pi x)+2\sin(5\pi x/3)& x\in[0,3]\\ u(0,t)=u(3,t)=0& t>0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}

Then \(L=3\) and \(c=2\).

Example

\begin{equation*} u(x,0)=\sin\frac{3\pi}{3}x+2\sin(\frac{5\pi}{3}x) \end{equation*}

So

\begin{align*} u(x,t)&=e^{-(\frac{2\cdot 3\pi}{3})^2t}\sin(\frac{3\pi}{3} x)+2e^{-(\frac{2\cdot 5\pi}{3})^2t}\sin(\frac{5\pi}{3}x)\\ &=e^{-(2\pi)^2t}\sin(\pi x)+2e^{-(\frac{10\pi}{3})^2t}\sin(\frac{5\pi}{3}x) \end{align*}

General Initial Condition

Want to solve

\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} \partial_tu(x,t)-c^2\partial_x^2u(x,t)=0&x\in[0,L],t>0\\ u(x,0)=f(x)&x\in[0,L]\\ u(0,t)=u(L,t)=0& t>0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}

for any \(f\).

General Solution

\(u(x,t)=\sum\limits_{k=1}^\infty A_kT_k(t)X_k(x)\)

  • Assume that
\begin{align*} \sum\limits_{k=1}^\infty A_kT_k(0)X_k(x)&=\sum\limits_{k=1}^\infty A_kX_k(x)\\ &=f(x) \end{align*}
  • Want to find the values of \(A_1,A_2,A_2,\cdots\) such that the above is true

Compute

\begin{align*} \int_0^L f(x) X_k(x)dx &= \int_0^L\big(\sum\limits_{j=1}^\infty A_jX_j(x)\big)X_k(x)dx\\ &=\sum\limits_{j=1}^\infty \big[A_j \int_0^LX_j(x)X_k(x)dx\big]\\ &=0+0+\cdots+0+A_k\frac{L}{2}+0+\cdots\\ &=A_k\frac{L}{2} \end{align*}

So

\begin{align*} A_k &=\frac{2}{L}\int_0^Lf(x)X_k(x)dx\\ &=\frac{2}{L}\int_0^Lf(x)\sin\big(\frac{k\pi}{L}x\big)dx \end{align*}

Put it All Together

\begin{align*} u(x,t) &= \sum\limits_{k=1}^\infty A_kT_k(t)X_k(x)\\ &=\sum\limits_{k=1}^\infty \frac{2}{L}\bigg[\int_0^Lf(s)X_k(s)ds\bigg]T_k(t)X_k(x)\\ &=\sum\limits_{k=1}^\infty \frac{2}{L}\bigg[\int_0^Lf(s)\sin\big(\frac{k\pi}{L}s\big)ds\bigg] e^{-\big(\frac{ck\pi}{L}\big)^2t}\sin\big(\frac{k\pi}{L}x\big) \end{align*}

Why did this work??

Mostly because if \(j\neq k\) then

\begin{equation*} \int_0^L X_j(x)X_k(x)=0 \end{equation*}

Orthogonality

Let \((V,\langle,\rangle)\) be an inner product space. A collection \(\{\varphi_1,\varphi_2,\cdots\}\) is orthogonal if

\begin{equation*} \langle \varphi_j,\varphi_k\rangle = 0 \end{equation*}

whenever \(j\neq k\).

Orthogonality Example

Recall, that for two functions \(f,g\in C([0,L])\), then

\begin{equation*} \langle f,g\rangle_{L^2}=\int_0^Lf(s)g(s)ds \end{equation*}

Let \(\{X_1,X_2,...\}\subset C([0,L])\) be as before. Then it is an orthogonal set with respect to the \(L^2\) inner product.

Properties of Orthogonal Sets

Let \((V,\langle,\rangle)\) be an inner product space and \(\{\varphi_1,\varphi_2,\cdots\}\) be an orthogonal set. If

\begin{equation*} f=\sum\limits_{k=1}^\infty A_k\varphi_k \end{equation*}

Then \(A_k=\frac{\langle f,\varphi_k\rangle}{\langle \varphi_k,\varphi_k\rangle}\).

Proof

\begin{align*} \langle f,\varphi_k\rangle &= \langle \sum\limits_{j=1}^\infty A_j\varphi_j,\varphi_k\rangle\\ &=\sum\limits A_j\langle \varphi_j,\varphi_k\rangle\\ &=0+0+\cdots+0+ A_k\langle \varphi_k,\varphi_k\rangle+0+\cdots\\ &=A_k\langle\varphi_k,\varphi_k\rangle \end{align*}

So \(A_k=\frac{\langle f,\varphi_k\rangle}{\langle \varphi_k,\varphi_k\rangle}\).

Buy WHY are the \(X_k\) Orthogonal?

Definition: Let \((V,\langle,\rangle)\) be an inner product space and \(W\subset V\) be a subspace. Let \(T:V\to V\) be such that

\begin{equation*} \langle T w_1,w_2\rangle=\langle w_1, T w_2\rangle \end{equation*}

for all \(w_1,w_2\in W\). Then we say that \(T\) is symmetric on \(W\). If \(W=V\), we just say that \(T\) is symmetric.

Symmetry of Second Derivative

Let

\begin{equation*} D([0,L])=\{f\in C^\infty([0,L])|f(0)=f(L)=0\} \end{equation*}

(\(D\) stands for “Dirichlet”). Let \(T:C^\infty([0,L])\to C^\infty([0,L])\) be defined by

\begin{equation*} T(f)=f'' \end{equation*}

Then \(T\) is symmetric on \(D([0,L])\) (with respect to the \(L^2\) inner product).

Proof (Integration by Parts)

Let \(f,g\in D([0,L])\).

\begin{align*} \langle Tf,g\rangle_{L^2}&=\int_0^Lf''gds\\ &= f'g\big|_0^L-\int_0^Lf'g'ds\\ &= (f'g-g'f)\big|_0^L+\int_0^Lfg''ds\\ &= (f'g-g'f)\big|_0^L+\langle f,Tg\rangle_{L^2} \end{align*}

But \(f,g\in D([0,L])\), so \((f'g-g'f)\big|_0^L=0\). Thus,

\begin{equation*} \langle Tf,g\rangle_{L^2}=\langle f,Tg\rangle_{L^2} \end{equation*}

Analysis

What does it mean to be a solution to

\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} X''&=\lambda X\\ X(0)&=0\\ X(L)&=0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}

It means that \(X\) is an eigenvector (eigenfunction) of the second derivative, and \(X\in D([0,L])\).

Eigenvectors of Symmetric Transformations

Let \((V,\langle,\rangle)\) be an inner product space and \(T:V\to V\) be symmetric on \(W\subset V\). Let \(\varphi_1,\varphi_2\in W\) be such that

  • \(T\varphi_1=\lambda_1\varphi_1\)
  • \(T\varphi_2=\lambda_2\varphi_2\)
  • \(\lambda_1\neq \lambda_2\)

Then, \(\langle \varphi_1,\varphi_2\rangle =0\).

Proof

\begin{align*} \langle T\varphi_1,\varphi_2\rangle &= \langle \lambda_1\varphi_1,\varphi_2\rangle\\ &=\lambda_1\langle\varphi_1,\varphi_2\rangle\\ &=\langle \varphi_1, T \varphi_2\rangle\\ &=\langle\varphi_1,\lambda_2\varphi_2\rangle\\ &=\lambda_2\langle\varphi_1,\varphi_2\rangle \end{align*}

So

\begin{align*} \lambda_1\langle \varphi_1,\varphi_2\rangle &= \lambda_2\langle \varphi_1,\varphi_2\rangle\\ (\lambda_1-\lambda_2)\langle\varphi_1,\varphi_2\rangle&=0\\ \langle \varphi_1,\varphi_2\rangle &=0 \end{align*}

But WHY Are the Eigenvalues Non-Positive?

Definition: Let \((V,\langle,\rangle)\) be an inner product space and \(T:V\to V\) be linear. Let \(W\subset V\) be a subspace. If, for all \(w\in W\), we have

\begin{equation*} \langle Tw,w\rangle \le 0 \end{equation*}

Then, we say that \(T\) is non-positive on \(W\).

The Second Derivative is Non-Positive

Let \(T:C^\infty([0,L])\to C^\infty([0,L])\) be defined by \(T(f)=f''\). Then \(T\) is non-positive on \(D([0,L])\) (with respect to the \(L^2\) inner product).

Proof (Integration by Parts)

Let \(f\in D([0,L])\). Then,

\begin{align*} \langle Tf,f\rangle_{L^2} &= \int_0^L f''fds\\ &= f'f\big|_0^L-\int_0^Lf'f'ds \end{align*}

But, \(f\in D([0,L])\), so \(f'f\big|_0^L=0\). Thus

\begin{align*} \langle Tf,f\rangle_{L^2} &= -\int_0^L(f')^2ds\\ &\le 0 \end{align*}

Eigenvalues of Non-Positive Transformations

Let \((V,\langle,\rangle)\) be an inner product space. Let \(T:V\to V\) be non-positive on \(W\subset V\). If \(\varphi\in W\) is non-zero and \(T\varphi=\lambda\varphi\), then \(\lambda\le 0\).

Proof

\begin{align*} \langle T\varphi,\varphi\rangle &= \langle \lambda\varphi,\varphi\rangle\\ &=\lambda\langle \varphi,\varphi\rangle\\ &\le 0 \end{align*}

But \(\langle \varphi,\varphi\rangle >0\). Thus, \(\lambda\le 0\).
