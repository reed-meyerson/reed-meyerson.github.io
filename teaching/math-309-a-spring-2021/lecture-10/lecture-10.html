Real Eigenvalues

Eigenvalues \(\lambda_1,\lambda_2\)

  • \(\lambda_1>0,\lambda_2>0\)
    • unstable node (everything points out)
  • \(\lambda_1<0,\lambda_2<0\)
    • asymptotically stable node (everything poitns in)
  • \(\lambda_1>0,\lambda_2<0\)
    • unstable saddle point

Complex Eigenvalues

If \(\lambda_1=a+bi\) and \(\lambda_2=a-bi\) with \(b\neq 0\)

  • \(a>0\)
    • unstable (outward) spiral
  • \(a<0\)
    • asymptotically stable (inward) spiral
  • \(a=0\)
    • stable periodic orbits

Direction of Spiral/Orbits

If \(A=\begin{pmatrix} A_{11} & A_{12}\\ A_{21} & A_{22}\end{pmatrix}\)

  • If \(A_{21}>0\) then counterclockwise
  • If \(A_{21}<0\) then clockwise

Solution Operators

Observation: Let \(x_0,y_0\in\mathbb{R}^n\) be initial conditions, and \(c\in\mathbb{R}\). Let \(A\) be an \(n\times n\) matrix. Let

\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} x'&=Ay\\ x(0)&=x_0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} y'&=Ay\\ y(0)&=y_0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}

If \(z(t)=cx(t)+y(t)\) then \(z\) is the solution to

\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} z'&=Az\\ z(0)&=cx_0+y_0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}

Input \(\to\) Solution is Linear

  • \([\text{initial condition}]\to[\text{solve ODE}]\to [\text{solution}]\)

defines a linear transformation from \(\mathbb{R}^n\) to \(C^1(\mathbb{R};\mathbb{R}^n)\).

  • What is this linear transformation?
    • Answer will have something to do with \(e^{At}\) (exponential of a matrix???)

Motivation for Matrix Exponential

If \(A\in\mathbb{R}\) (just a number) and \(x_0\in\mathbb{R}\) (just a number) then the solution to

\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} x'&=Ax\\ x(0)&=x_0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}

Is \(x(t)=e^{At}x_0\), so \(x_0\mapsto e^{At}x_0\) describes “solution operator”.

Exponential Power Series

Recall, if \(A\) is a number, then

\begin{align*} e^{A}&=\sum\limits_{k=0}^\infty \frac{A^k}{k!}\\ &=1+A+\frac{A^2}{2}+\frac{A^3}{6}+\cdots \end{align*}

Powers of Matrices

If \(A\) is an \(n\times n\) matrix

\begin{align*} A^0&=I\\ A^1&=A\\ A^2&=A\cdot A\\ A^3&=A\cdot A\cdot A\\ &\vdots \end{align*}

What happens if we plug a matrix into a power series?

Matrix Exponential Definition

Let \(A\) be an \(n\times n\) matrix. Define

\begin{align*} e^{A}&=\sum\limits_{k=0}^\infty \frac{A^k}{k!}\\ &=1+A+\frac{A^2}{2}+\frac{A^3}{6}+\cdots\\ &=\lim\limits_{N\to\infty}\sum\limits_{k=0}^N\frac{A^k}{k!} \end{align*}

How do we compute this?

Polynomials of Matrices

Let \(p(x)=a_0x^0+a_1x^1+...+a_mx^m\). Define

\begin{equation*} p(A)=a_0A^0+a_1A^1+...+a_mA^m \end{equation*}

Example

Let \(p(x)=3+2x^2\) and \(A=\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2\\ 3 & 4\end{pmatrix}\).

\begin{align*} A^0&=\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0\\ 0& 1\end{pmatrix}\\ A^2&=\begin{pmatrix} 7 & 10\\15 & 22\end{pmatrix}\\ p(A)&=3A^0+A^2\\ &=\begin{pmatrix} 3 & 0\\ 0& 3\end{pmatrix}+\begin{pmatrix} 7 & 10\\15 & 22\end{pmatrix}\\ &=\begin{pmatrix} 10 & 10\\15 & 25\end{pmatrix} \end{align*}

Powers of Diagonal Matrix

Let \(D=\begin{pmatrix} \lambda_1 & 0\\ 0 & \lambda_2\end{pmatrix}\). Then

\begin{align*} D^k&=\begin{pmatrix} \lambda_1 & 0\\ 0 & \lambda_2\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} \lambda_1 & 0\\ 0 & \lambda_2\end{pmatrix}...\begin{pmatrix} \lambda_1 & 0\\ 0 & \lambda_2\end{pmatrix}\\ &= \begin{pmatrix} \lambda_1^k & 0\\ 0 & \lambda_2^k\end{pmatrix} \end{align*}

Polynomials of Diagonal Matrices

Let \(D=\begin{pmatrix} \lambda_1 & 0\\ 0 & \lambda_2\end{pmatrix}\), and \(p(x)=a_0+a_1x+...+a_mx^m\).

\begin{align*} p(D)&=a_0\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0\\ 0 & 1\end{pmatrix}+a_1\begin{pmatrix}\lambda_1 & 0\\ 0 & \lambda_2\end{pmatrix}+...+a_m\begin{pmatrix}\lambda^m_1 & 0\\ 0 & \lambda^m_2\end{pmatrix}\\ &=\begin{pmatrix} a_0+a_1\lambda_1+...+a_m\lambda_1^m & 0\\ 0 & a_0+a_1\lambda_2+...+a_m\lambda_2^m\end{pmatrix}\\ &=\begin{pmatrix} p(\lambda_1) & 0\\ 0 & p(\lambda_2)\end{pmatrix} \end{align*}

Exponential of Diagonal Matrices

Let \(D=\begin{pmatrix} \lambda_1 & 0\\ 0 & \lambda_2\end{pmatrix}\), and \(p_N(x)=\sum\limits_{k=0}^N\frac{x^k}{k!}\). Then

\begin{align*} e^{D}&=\lim\limits_{N\to\infty}p_N(D)\\ &=\lim\limits_{N\to\infty}\begin{pmatrix} p_N(\lambda_1) & 0\\ 0 & p_N(\lambda_2)\end{pmatrix}\\ &=\begin{pmatrix} e^{\lambda_1} & 0\\ 0 & e^{\lambda_2}\end{pmatrix} \end{align*}

With “Time”

Exercise: Let \(t\in\mathbb{R}\) and \(D=\begin{pmatrix} \lambda_1 & 0\\ 0 & \lambda_2\end{pmatrix}\). Then

\begin{equation*} e^{Dt}=\begin{pmatrix} e^{\lambda_1t} & 0\\0 & e^{\lambda_2t}\end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}

Powers of Diagonalizable Matrices

Let \(D\) be an \(n\times n\) matrix, \(P\) be an invertible \(n\times n\) matrix, then

\begin{align*} (PDP^{-1})^k&=PDP^{-1}PDP^{-1}...PDP^{-1}\\ &=PD(P^{-1}P)D(P^{-1}P)...(P^{-1}P)DP^{-1}\\ &=PDIDI...IDP^{-1}\\ &=PDD...DP^{-1}\\ &=PD^kP^{-1} \end{align*}

Similarly \((P^{-1}DP)^k=P^{-1}D^kP\).

Polynomials of Diagonalizable Matrices

Let \(D\) be an \(n\times n\) matrix, \(P\) be an invertible \(n\times n\) matrix, and \(p(x)=a_0+a_1x+...+a_mx^m\). Then

\begin{align*} p(PDP^{-1})&= a_0(PDP^{-1})^0+a_1(PDP^{-1})^1+...+a_m(PDP^{-1})^m\\ &=a_0PD^0P^{-1}+a_1PD^1P^{-1}+...+a_mPD^mP^{-1}\\ &=Pa_0D^0P^{-1}+Pa_1D^1P^{-1}+...+Pa_mD^mP^{-1}\\ &=P(a_0D^0P^{-1}+a_1D^1P^{-1}+...+a_mD^mP^{-1})\\ &=P(a_0D^0+a_1D^1+...+a_mD^m)P^{-1}\\ &=Pp(D)P^{-1} \end{align*}

Similarly \(p(P^{-1}DP)=P^{-1}p(D)P\).

Polynomials of Diagonalizable Matrices

As an example, let \(A=\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 5 & 0\\ 0 & 6\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4\end{pmatrix}^{-1}\), and \(p(x)=2+x^2\). Then

\begin{align*} p(A)&=\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 2+5^2 & 0\\ 0 & 2+6^2\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4\end{pmatrix}^{-1}\\ &=\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 27 & 0\\ 0 & 38\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4\end{pmatrix}^{-1} \end{align*}

Exponential of a Diagonalizable Matrix

Let \(A=PDP^{-1}\). Let \(p_N(x)=\sum\limits_{k=0}^N\frac{x^k}{k!}\). Then

\begin{align*} e^{A} &= \lim\limits_{N\to\infty}p_N(A)\\ &=\lim\limits_{N\to\infty}Pp_N(D)P^{-1}\\ &=Pe^{D}P^{-1} \end{align*}

Similarly \(e^{P^{-1}DP}=P^{-1}e^DP\)

With “Time”

Exercise: Let \(A=PDP^{-1}\). Then

\begin{equation*} e^{At}=Pe^{Dt}P^{-1} \end{equation*}

Example

Let \(A=\begin{pmatrix} 7 & -6\\8 & -7\end{pmatrix}\). Compute \(e^{At}\).

Diagonalize

\(A=\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 3\\ 1 & 4\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0\\ 0 & -1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 3\\ 1 & 4\end{pmatrix}^{-1}\), so

\begin{equation*} e^{At}=\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 3\\ 1 & 4\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} e^{1t} & 0\\ 0 & e^{-1t}\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 3\\ 1 & 4\end{pmatrix}^{-1} \end{equation*}
