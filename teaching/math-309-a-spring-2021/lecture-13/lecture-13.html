Heat Equation with Dirichlet Boundary Conditions

\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} \partial_tu(x,t)-c^2\partial_x^2u(x,t)=0&x\in[0,L],t>0\\ u(x,0)=f(x)&x\in[0,L]\\ u(0,t)=u(L,t)=0& t>0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}

“PDE” Part

\begin{equation*} \partial_tu(x,t)-c^2\partial_x^2u(x,t)=0\text{, }x\in[0,L],t>0 \end{equation*}

Initial Condition

\begin{equation*} u(x,0)=f(x)\text{, }x\in[0,L] \end{equation*}

Boundary Conditions

\begin{equation*} u(0,t)=u(L,t)=0\text{, }t>0 \end{equation*}
  • “Dirichlet” boundary conditions

Linearity of PDE Part

Let \(u_1\), \(u_2\) be solutions to

\begin{equation*} \partial_tu(x,t)-c^2\partial_x^2u(x,t)=0\text{, }x\in[0,L],t>0 \end{equation*}

and \(\alpha\in\mathbb{R}\). Then, so is \((\alpha u_1+u_2)\).

Proof

\begin{align*} \partial_t(\alpha u_1+u_2)-c^2\partial_x^2(\alpha u_1+u_2)&=\alpha \partial_t u_1+\partial_t u_2-\alpha c^2\partial_x^2u_1-c^2\partial_x^2u_2\\ &=\alpha \big[\partial_t u_1-c^2\partial_x^2 u_1\big]+\big[\partial_t u_2-c^2\partial_x^2 u_2\big]\\ &=\alpha\cdot 0+0\\ &=0 \end{align*}

Linearity of Boundary Conditions

Let \(u_1,u_2\) satisfy the boundary conditions

\begin{equation*} u(0,t)=u(L,t)=0\text{, }t>0 \end{equation*}

and \(\alpha\in\mathbb{R}\). Then \((\alpha u_1+u_2)\) also satisfies the above boundary conditions.

Proof

\begin{align*} (\alpha u_1+u_2)(0,t) &= \alpha u_1(0,t)+u_2(0,t)\\ &= \alpha\cdot 0+0\\ &= 0\\ (\alpha u_1+u_2)(L,t) &= \alpha u_1(L,t)+u_2(L,t)\\ &= \alpha\cdot 0+0\\ &= 0 \end{align*}

Linearity of PDE with Boundary Conditions

Let \(u_1,u_2\) satisfy

\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} \partial_tu(x,t)-c^2\partial_x^2u(x,t)=0&x\in[0,L],t>0\\ u(0,t)=u(L,t)=0& t>0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}

and \(\alpha\in\mathbb{R}\). Then \((\alpha u_1+u_2)\) also sastisfies the above constraints.

Effect of Linearity on Initial Conditions

Let \(u_1(x,0)=f(x)\) and \(u_2(x,0)=g(x)\). Then

\begin{equation*} (\alpha u_1+u_2)(x,0)=\alpha f(x)+g(x) \end{equation*}

Whole PDE

Let \(u_1\) satisfy

\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} \partial_tu(x,t)-c^2\partial_x^2u(x,t)=0&x\in[0,L],t>0\\ u(x,0)=f(x)&x\in[0,L]\\ u(0,t)=u(L,t)=0& t>0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}

and \(u_2\) satisfy

\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} \partial_tu(x,t)-c^2\partial_x^2u(x,t)=0&x\in[0,L],t>0\\ u(x,0)=g(x)&x\in[0,L]\\ u(0,t)=u(L,t)=0& t>0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}

and \(\alpha\in\mathbb{R}\). Then \((\alpha u_1+u_2)\) satisfies

\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} \partial_tu(x,t)-c^2\partial_x^2u(x,t)=0&x\in[0,L],t>0\\ u(x,0)=(\alpha f+g)(x)&x\in[0,L]\\ u(0,t)=u(L,t)=0& t>0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}

Strategy

Find a “bunch” of solutions to

\begin{equation*} (\star) \begin{cases} \partial_tu(x,t)-c^2\partial_x^2u(x,t)=0&x\in[0,L],t>0\\ u(0,t)=u(L,t)=0& t>0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}

Then add them up so we can “match” any initial condition

Separation of Variables

Ansatz: Assume \(u(x,t)=T(t)X(x)\)

  • examples \(\sin(x)\cos(t)\), \(e^tx^2\)
  • NOT \(\sin(xt)\)

Also assume that \(u(x,t)\) is non-trivial

  • not identically zero

Now, plug into \((\star)\)

Plug into PDE Part

\begin{align*} \partial_tu(x,t)-c^2\partial_x^2u(x,t)&=\partial_t\big[T(t)X(x)\big]-c^2\partial_x^2\big[T(t)X(x)]\\ &= T'(t)X(x)-c^2T(t)X''(x)\\ &=0 \end{align*}

So

\begin{align*} \frac{1}{c^2}T'(t)X(x)&=T(t)X''(x)\\ \frac{T'X}{c^2TX}&=\frac{TX''}{TX}\\ \frac{T'}{c^2T}&=\frac{X''}{X} \end{align*}

Eigenvalues

Let \(x_0\in[0,L]\) be such that \(X(x_0)\neq 0\). Then let \(\lambda =\frac{X''(x_0)}{X(x_0)}\).

\begin{align*} \frac{T'(t)}{c^2T(t)}&=\frac{X''(x_0)}{X(x_0)}\\ &=\lambda\\ &=\frac{X''(x)}{X(x)} \end{align*}

so

\begin{equation*} \frac{T'}{c^2T}=\frac{X''}{X}=\lambda \end{equation*}

Eigenvalue Problem

Thus, if \(u=TX\) satisfies the PDE it must satisfy the following constraints

\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} T'&=c^2\lambda T\\ X''&=\lambda X \end{cases} \end{equation*}

Plug into Boundary Conditions

\begin{align*} u(0,t)&=T(t)X(0)\\ &=0 \end{align*}

Let \(T(t_0)\neq 0\). Thus, \(X(0)=0\). Similarly

\begin{align*} u(L,t)&=T(t)X(L)\\ &=0 \end{align*}

implies that \(X(L)=0\)

Whole Eigenvalue Problem

\begin{equation*} (\star\star) \begin{cases} T'&=c^2\lambda T\\ X''&=\lambda X\\ X(0)&=0\\ X(L)&=0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}

A solution is a triple \((X,T,\lambda)\)

Solving the Eigenvalue Problem

Scaling Properties of Solutions

If \((X,T,\lambda)\) is a solution to \((\star\star)\), and \(\alpha\in\mathbb{R}\), then so is

  • \((\alpha X,T,\lambda)\)
  • \((X,\alpha T,\lambda)\)
  • \((\alpha X,\alpha T,\lambda)\)

Since we will be scaling these solutions and adding them, we will allow ourselves to pick one “rescaling” for each family of solutions.

Time Part

\(T'=c^2\lambda T\implies T(t)=e^{c^2\lambda t}\)

Spatial Part

\begin{equation*} (\star\star\star) \begin{cases} X''&=\lambda X\\ X(0)&=0\\ X(L)&=0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}

Three cases

  • \(\lambda =\gamma^2>0\)
  • \(\lambda =0\)
  • \(\lambda =-\gamma^2<0\)

Case (1) \(\lambda >0\)

If \(\lambda=\gamma^2>0\) and \(X''=\gamma^2 X\), then

\begin{equation*} X(x)=C_1e^{\gamma x}+C_2e^{-\gamma x} \end{equation*}

Case (1) Boundary Values

\begin{align*} X(0)&=C_1e^0+C_2e^0\\ &=C_1+C_2\\ &=0\\ X(L)&=C_1e^{\gamma L}+C_2e^{-\gamma L}\\ &=0 \end{align*}

\(\implies C_2=-C_1\) so

\begin{align*} C_1e^{\gamma L}-C_1e^{-\gamma L}&=0\\ C_1(e^{\gamma L}-e^{-\gamma L})&=0\\ C_1&=0\\ C_2&=0 \end{align*}

Only Trivial Solutions

Thus, \(C_1=C_2=0\), so \(X(t)=0\) is the trivial solution.

Case (2) \(\lambda =0\)

If \(\lambda =0\) and \(X''=0X\), then

\begin{equation*} X(x)=C_1x+C_2 \end{equation*}

Case (2) Boundary Values

\begin{align*} X(0)&=C_10+C_2\\ &=0\\ X(L)&=C_1L+C_2\\ &=C_1L+0\\ &=0 \end{align*}

So, \(C_1=C_2=0\) and \(X\) is the trivial solution again.

Case (3) \(\lambda<0\)

If \(\lambda=-\gamma^2<0\), and \(X''=-\gamma^2X\), then

\begin{equation*} X(x)=C_1\cos(\gamma x)+C_2\sin(\gamma x)\\ \end{equation*}

Case (3) Boundary Values

\begin{align*} X(0)&=C_1\cos 0+C_2\sin 0\\ &=C_1\\ &=0\\ X(L)&=C_1\cos\gamma L+C_2\sin \gamma L\\ &=C_2\sin\gamma L\\ &= 0 \end{align*}

If \(C_2=0\), then trivial solution. So assume \(C_2\neq 0\). Then

\begin{equation*} \sin\gamma L=0 \end{equation*}

Thus, \(\gamma L=k\pi\), (i.e. \(\gamma=\frac{k\pi}{L}\)) for any integer \(k\). So \(\lambda =-\big(\frac{k\pi}{L}\big)^2\).

  • Assumed \(\lambda \neq 0\), so \(k\neq 0\).
  • If \(-\big(\frac{-k\pi}{L}\big)^2=-\big(\frac{k\pi}{L}\big)^2\), so can throw out the negative values of \(k\).

Result

Every solution to \((\star\star)\) is of the form

  • \((\sin(\frac{k\pi}{L} x),e^{-(\frac{ck\pi}{L})^2t},-(\frac{k\pi}{L})^2)\), for \(k=1,2,3,\cdots\)

Solutions to the PDE

Thus, let \(u_k(x,t)=e^{-(\frac{ck\pi}{L})^2t}\sin(\frac{k\pi}{L} x)\) for \(k=1,2,3,\cdots\)

  • \(u_k\) solves \((\star)\).
