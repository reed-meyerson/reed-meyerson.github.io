Dot Product Review

Let \(v,w\in\mathbb{R}^n\) such that

\begin{align*} v&=(v_1,...,v_n)\\ w&=(w_1,...,w_n) \end{align*}

Then

\begin{align*} v\bullet w&=v_1w_1+v_2w_2+...+v_nw_n\\ &=\sum\limits_{k=1}^nv_iw_i \end{align*}

Algebraic Properties

Let \(v,w,z\in\mathbb{R}^n\) and \(\alpha\in\mathbb{R}\). Then

  • SYMMETRIC

    \begin{equation*} v\bullet w=w\bullet v \end{equation*}

  • LINEAR IN THE FIRST ARGUMENT

    \begin{equation*} (\alpha v+w)\bullet z=\alpha (v\bullet z)+(w\bullet z) \end{equation*}

Analytic Properties

Let \(v\in\mathbb{R}\). Then

  • POSITIVE DEFINITE
\begin{equation*} \text{If }v\neq 0\text{ then }(v\bullet v)>0 \end{equation*}

Geometric Properties

Define \(|v|=\sqrt{v\bullet v}\)

  • Length \(\implies\) \(|v|\)
  • Distance \(\implies\) \(|v-w|\)
  • Angle \(\implies\) \(\theta=\cos^{-1}\frac{v\bullet w}{|v||w|}\)
    • Orthogonality \(v\bullet w=0\implies v\perp w\)

Derived Algebraic Properties

Let \(v,w,z\in\mathbb{R}^n\) and \(\alpha\in\mathbb{R}\). Then

\begin{equation*} v\bullet (\alpha w+z)=\alpha(v\bullet w)+(v\bullet z) \end{equation*}
  • LINEAR IN THE SECOND ARGUMENT

Proof

\begin{align*} v\bullet (\alpha w+z)&=(\alpha w+z)\bullet v&\text{symmetry}\\ &=\alpha (w\bullet v) + (z\bullet v)&\text{linear in first}\\ &=\alpha(v\bullet w)+(v\bullet z)&\text{symmetry} \end{align*}

More Derived Properties

Let \(v\in\mathbb{R}^n\). Then \(\vec{0}\bullet v=0\).

Proof

\begin{align*} \vec{0}\bullet v &= (\vec{0}+\vec{0})\bullet v& \text{property of }\vec{0}\\ &= (\vec{0}\bullet v)+(\vec{0}\bullet v) &\text{linear in first}\\ \end{align*}

So

\begin{align*} 0&=\vec{0}\bullet v& \text{subtraction} \end{align*}

Derived geometric properties

Let \(v,w,z\in\mathbb{R}^n\) and \(\alpha\in\mathbb{R}\). Then

  • \(|\alpha v|=|\alpha| |v|\)
  • \(|v+w|\le |v| + |w|\)
  • \(|v-z|\le |v-w| + |w-z|\)

The above properties can all be derived using only SYMMETRY, LINEAR IN THE FIRST, and POSITIVE DEFINITE (and linear algebra)

Proof \(|\alpha v|=|\alpha| |v|\)

\begin{align*} |\alpha v|&=\sqrt{(\alpha v)\bullet (\alpha v)} & \text{definition of }|\cdot|\\ &=\sqrt{\alpha^2(v\bullet v)} & \text{linear in both}\\ &=\sqrt{\alpha^2}\sqrt{v\bullet v}& \text{property of $\sqrt{\cdot}$}\\ &=|\alpha|\sqrt{v\bullet v}& \sqrt{x^2}=|x| \end{align*}

Inner Products

Let \(V\) be a vector space. An inner product on \(V\) is a rule \(\langle,\rangle\) which assigns to each pair of vectors \(v,w\in V\) a real number \(\langle v,w\rangle\). We require \(\langle,\rangle\) to satisfy

  • SYMMETRIC

    If \(v,w\in V\), then \(\langle v,w\rangle =\langle w,v\rangle\)

  • LINEAR IN THE FIRST ARGUMENT

    If \(v,w\in V\) and \(\alpha\in\mathbb{R}\), then \(\langle \alpha v + w,z \rangle=\alpha\langle v,z\rangle + \langle w,z\rangle\)

  • POSITIVE DEFINITE

    If \(v\in V\) and \(v\neq 0\), then \(\langle v,v\rangle >0\).

Inner Products Examples

For vectors \(v,w\in\mathbb{R}^n\), define

\begin{equation*} \langle v,w\rangle_{\mathbb{R}^n}=v\bullet w \end{equation*}

Then, \(\langle,\rangle_{\mathbb{R}^n}\) is an inner product on \(\mathbb{R}^n\).

Inner Products Examples

For vectors \(v=(v_1,v_2)\) and \(w=(w_1,w_2)\) in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) define

\begin{equation*} \langle v,w\rangle_2=v_1w_1+2v_2w_2 \end{equation*}

Then \(\langle,\rangle_2\) is an inner product.

Proof of Symmetry

\begin{align*} \langle v,w\rangle_2&=v_1w_1+2v_2w_2\\ &=w_1v_1+2w_2v_2\\ &=\langle w,v\rangle_2 \end{align*}

Proof of Linearity in First Argument

Let \(z=(z_1,z_2)\) and \(\alpha\in\mathbb{R}\).

\begin{align*} \langle \alpha v+w,z\rangle_2&=\langle(\alpha v_1+w_1,\alpha v_2+w_2),(z_1,z_2)\rangle_2\\ &=(\alpha v_1+w_1)z_1+2(\alpha v_2+w_2)z_2\\ &=\alpha(v_1z_1+2v_2z_2)+(w_1z_1+2w_2z_2)\\ &=\alpha\langle v,z\rangle_2+\langle w,z\rangle_2 \end{align*}

Proof of Positive Definiteness

Let \(v=(v_1,v_2)\neq (0,0)\). Then

\begin{align*} \langle v,v\rangle_2&=v_1^2+2v_2^2\\ &> 0 \end{align*}

The \(L^2\) Inner Product

For \(f,g\in C([a,b])\), define

\begin{equation*} \langle f,g\rangle_{L^2}=\int_a^bf(s)g(s)ds \end{equation*}

Then \(\langle,\rangle_{L^2}\) defines and inner product on \(C([a,b])\).

  • If we want to be specific about the interval, we write \(L^2([a,b])\).

Proof of Symmetry

\begin{align*} \langle f,g\rangle_{L^2}&=\int_a^bf(s)g(s)ds\\ &=\int_a^bg(s)f(s)ds\\ &=\langle g,f\rangle_{L^2} \end{align*}

Proof of Linearity in First Argument

\begin{align*} \langle \alpha f+g,h\rangle_{L^2}&=\int_a^b(\alpha f+g)(s)h(s)ds\\ &=\int_a^b[\alpha f(s)+g(s)]h(s)ds\\ &=\alpha\int_a^bf(s)h(s)ds+\int_a^bg(s)h(s)ds\\ &=\alpha\langle f,h\rangle_{L^2}+\langle g,h\rangle_{L^2} \end{align*}

Proof of Positive Definiteness

Let be \(f\in C([a,b])\) be such that \(f\) is not identically zero (i.e. not the zero function).

\begin{align*} \langle f,f\rangle_{L^2}&=\int_a^bf(s)f(s)ds\\ &=\int_a^b[f(s)]^2ds\\ &\ge 0 \end{align*}

Since \(f\) is not the zero function, the area under the curve of \(f^2(s)\) is strictly positive. Thus

\begin{equation*} \int_a^b[f(s)]^2ds>0 \end{equation*}

Inner Product Space

An inner product space is a pair \((V,\langle,\rangle)\) where \(V\) is a vector space and \(\langle,\rangle\) is an inner product on \(V\).

  • Practically, this just means there is a fixed inner product.

Orthogonality

Let \((V,\langle,\rangle)\) be an inner product space. Let \(v,w\in V\). If \(\langle v,w\rangle=0\), we say that \(v\) is orthogonal to \(w\) and we write \(v\perp w\).

If \(S\subset V\) is any subset such that, for any pair \(v,w\in S\) such that \(v\neq w\), we say that \(S\) is an orthogonal subset.

Orthogonality Example

  • Let \(\hat i=(1,0)\) and \(\hat j=(0,1)\). Then \(\hat i\) is orthogonal to \(\hat j\) with respect to \(\langle,\rangle_{\mathbb{R}^2}\).
  • Let \(S=\{(1,0,0),(0,1,1),(0,1,-1)\}\). Then \(S\) is an orthogonal subset of \(\mathbb{R}^3\) with respect to \(\langle,\rangle_{\mathbb{R}^3}\).

Orthogonal Functions

Let \(\varphi_k(x)=\sin(k\pi x)\) on the interval \([0,1]\). Then \(\{\varphi_1,\varphi_2,...,\}\) is orthogonal with respect to the \(L^2([0,1])\) inner product.

Proof

Let \(j\neq k\) be natural numbers. We must show that \(\langle \varphi_j,\varphi_k\rangle_{L^2([0,1])} =0\)

\begin{align*} \langle \varphi_j,\varphi_k\rangle_{L^2}&=\int_0^1\varphi_j(s)\varphi_k(s)ds\\ &=\int_0^1\sin(j\pi s)\sin(k\pi s)ds\\ &=\int_0^1\frac{1}{2}[\cos((j-k)\pi s)-\cos((j+k)\pi s)]ds\\ &=\frac{1}{2}[\frac{1}{(j-k)\pi}\sin((j-k)\pi s)-\frac{1}{(j+k)\pi}\sin((j+k)\pi s]\bigg|_0^1\\ &=0 \end{align*}

Where we have used \(\sin(a)\sin(b)=\frac{1}{2}[\cos(a-b)-\cos(a+b)]\)

Orthonormal

Let \((V,\langle,\rangle)\) be an inner product space. Let \(S\subset V\) be an orthonogonal subset. We say \(S\) is orthonormal if, for all \(v\in S\), we have \(\langle v,v\rangle =1\).

Orthonormal Example

  • Let \(\hat i=(1,0)\) and \(\hat j=(0,1)\). Then \(\{\hat i,\hat j\}\) is orthonormal with respect to \(\langle,\rangle_{\mathbb{R}^2}\).
  • Let \(S=\{(1,0,0),(0,\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}},\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}),(0,\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}},-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}})\}\). Then \(S\) is an orthogonal subset of \(\mathbb{R}^3\) with respect to \(\langle,\rangle_{\mathbb{R}^3}\).

Orthonormal Functions

Let \(\hat \varphi_k(x)=\sqrt{2}\sin(k\pi x)\) on the interval \([0,1]\). Then \(\{\hat \varphi_1,\hat \varphi_2,...,\}\) is orthonormal with respect to the \(L^2([0,1])\) inner product.

Proof

From previous computation, we know that if \(j\neq k\), then

\begin{align*} \langle \hat \varphi_j,\hat\varphi_k\rangle_{L^2} &=\langle \sqrt{2}\varphi_j,\sqrt{2}\varphi_k\rangle_{L^2}\\ &=2\langle\varphi_j,\varphi_k\rangle_{L^2}\\ &=0 \end{align*}

Thus, we just have to show that \(\langle \hat\varphi_k,\hat\varphi_k\rangle_{L^2}=1\).

Proof

We compute

\begin{align*} \langle \hat\varphi_k,\hat\varphi_k\rangle_{L^2} &=2\int_0^1\sin^2(k\pi s)ds\\ &=2\int_0^1\frac{1}{2}[1-\cos(2k\pi s)]ds\\ &=[s-\frac{1}{2k\pi}\sin(2k\pi s)]\bigg|_0^1\\ &=1 \end{align*}

Where we have used \(\sin^2(a)=\frac{1}{2}[1-\cos(2a)]\).
