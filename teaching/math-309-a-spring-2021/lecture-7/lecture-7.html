Plotting Complex Numbers

Real and Imaginary Parts

  • Let \(z=a+bi\).
  • Define \(\mathbb{R}\text{e}(z)=a\)
  • Define \(\mathbb{I}\text{m}(z)=b\)

For example

\begin{align*} \mathbb{R}\text{e}(3-4i)&=3\\ \mathbb{I}\text{m}(3-4i)&=-4\\ \end{align*}

Addition

\begin{equation*} (a+bi)+(c+di)=(a+c)+(b+d)i \end{equation*}

For example

\begin{equation*} (3+4i)+(5+6i)=8+12i \end{equation*}

Multiplication

\begin{align*} (a+bi)(c+di)&=ac+bci+adi+bdi^2\\ &=ac+bci+adi-bd\\ &=(ac-bd)+(bc+ad)i \end{align*}

For example

\begin{equation*} (1+2i)(3+4i)=-5+10i \end{equation*}

Magnitude

Compute Magnitude with Conjugation

Let \(z=a+bi\)

  • \(|z|=\sqrt{a^2+b^2}\)

Compute

\begin{align*} (a+bi)(a-bi)&=a^2-abi+abi-b^2i^2\\ &=a^2+b^2 &=|z|^2 \end{align*}

So

\begin{equation*} |z|^2=z\overline{z} \end{equation*}

Division

Idea: For a real number \(x\), then if \(y\) is a real number satisfying

\begin{equation*} xy=1 \end{equation*}

Then \(y=\frac{1}{x}\)

  • Define \(a \div b=a\cdot\frac{1}{b}\)
  • i.e. define the reciprocal, then define division to be multiplication by the reciprocal.

Division

Let \(z=a+bi\). Want to find \((c+di)\) such that \((a+bi)(c+di)=1+0i\).

  • Compute
\begin{align*} z\cdot \frac{\overline{z}}{|z|^2}&=\frac{z\overline{z}}{|z|^2}\\ &=\frac{|z|^2}{|z|^2}\\ &=1 \end{align*}

So \(\frac{1}{a+bi}=\frac{a-bi}{a^2+b^2}\)

Back to Complex Eigenvalues

\begin{align*} A&=\begin{pmatrix} -2 & 1\\ -1 & -2\end{pmatrix}\\ \lambda &= -2\pm i \end{align*}

Complex Eigenvectors

We want to solve

\begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} -2 & 1\\ -1 & -2\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} a \\ b\end{pmatrix}=(-2+i)\begin{pmatrix} a \\ b\end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}

Expand

\begin{align*} -2a+b&=(-2+i)a\\ -a-2b&=(-2+i)b \end{align*}

Simplify

\begin{align*} -ia+b&=0\\ -a-ib&=0 \end{align*}

Complex Eigenvectors

Multiply bottom equation by \(i\)

\begin{align*} -ia+b&=0\\ i\cdot(-a-ib&=0) \end{align*}

Yields

\begin{align*} -ia+b&=0\\ -ia+b&=0 \end{align*}

So the solution is anything satisfying \(b=ia\). Choose \(a=1\) then \(b=i\).

First Eigenspace

  • \(-2+i\) is an eigenvalue
  • \(E(-2+i)=\text{span}\{(1,i)\}\).

Repeat with \(\lambda=-2-i\)???

  • HECK NO!!!

Want to Solve

\begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} -2 & 1\\ -1 & -2\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} a \\ b\end{pmatrix}=(-2-i)\begin{pmatrix} a \\ b\end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}

This is the conjugate of

\begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} -2 & 1\\ -1 & -2\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} a \\ b\end{pmatrix}=(-2+i)\begin{pmatrix} a \\ b\end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}

To solve, just conjugate every step. The result will be the conjugate of the previous answer.

Second Eigenspace

  • \(-2-i\) is an eigenvalue
  • \(E(-2-i)=\text{span}\{(1,-i)\}\).
  • This will always work, because complex solutions to real polynomial equations always come in conjugate pairs.

Complex Diagonalization

\begin{equation*} A=\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1\\ i & -i\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} -2+i & 0\\ 0 & -2-i\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1\\ i & -i\end{pmatrix}^{-1} \end{equation*}

Initial Value Problem

Consider

\begin{align*} x'&=\begin{pmatrix} -2 & 1\\ -1 & -2\end{pmatrix}x\\ x(0)&=(6,8) \end{align*}

General solution is

\begin{equation*} x(t)=c_1e^{(-2+i)t}\begin{pmatrix}1\\ i\end{pmatrix}+c_2e^{(-2-i)t}\begin{pmatrix} 1\\-i\end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}

Solve for Initial Condition

\begin{align*} x(0)&=c_1\begin{pmatrix}1\\ i\end{pmatrix}+c_2\begin{pmatrix} 1\\-i\end{pmatrix}\\ &=\begin{pmatrix}6\\8\end{pmatrix} \end{align*}

So

\begin{align*} c_1+c_2&=6\\ c_1i-c_2i&=8 \end{align*}

Keep Solving

Multiply bottom by \(i\)

\begin{align*} c_1+c_2&=6\\ i(c_1i-c_2i&=8) \end{align*}

Yields

\begin{align*} c_1+c_2&=6\\ -c_1+c_2&=i8 \end{align*}

Add equations

\begin{equation*} 2c_2=6+8i \end{equation*}

So \(c_2=3+4i\)

Keep Solving

Plug into top equation gives

\begin{equation*} c_1+(3+4i)=6 \end{equation*}

So \(c_1=3-4i\)

Write Down Final Solution

\(x(t)=(3-4i)e^{(-2+i)t}\begin{pmatrix} 1\\i\end{pmatrix}+(3+4i)e^{(-2-i)t}\begin{pmatrix} 1\\-i\end{pmatrix}\)

Wait a Minute

\begin{align*} x'&=\begin{pmatrix} -2 & 1\\ -1 & -2\end{pmatrix}x\\ x(0)&=(6,8) \end{align*}

Is a “Real” IVP. Just “follow the arrows”, so solution should be real. But

\begin{equation*} x(t)=(3-4i)e^{(-2+i)t}\begin{pmatrix}1\\ i\end{pmatrix}+(3+4i)e^{(-2-i)t}\begin{pmatrix} 1\\-i\end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}

looks complex.

Lets Investigate

\begin{equation*} x(t)=(3-4i)e^{(-2+i)t}\begin{pmatrix}1\\ i\end{pmatrix}+(3+4i)e^{(-2-i)t}\begin{pmatrix} 1\\-i\end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}

Let

\begin{align*} f(t)&=(3-4i)e^{(-2+i)t}\begin{pmatrix}1\\ i\end{pmatrix}\\ g(t)&=(3+4i)e^{(-2-i)t}\begin{pmatrix} 1\\-i\end{pmatrix} \end{align*}

Then \(f(t)=\overline{g(t)}\), so \(f(t)+\overline{f(t)}\) is real.
