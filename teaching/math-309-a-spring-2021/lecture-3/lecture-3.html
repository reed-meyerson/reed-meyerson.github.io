Goals for Today

Example 1

  • Let \(v,w\in\mathbb{R}^n\), \(\alpha\in\mathbb{R}\), and \(A\) be an \(m\times n\) matrix.

  • Then \((\alpha v+w)\in\mathbb{R}^n\), and

    \begin{equation*} A(\alpha v+w)=\alpha Av+Aw \end{equation*}

Example 2

  • Let \(f,g:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}\) be differentiable, and \(\alpha\in\mathbb{R}\).
  • Then \((\alpha f+g):\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}\) is differentiable, and
\begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dt}(\alpha f+g)=\alpha \frac{d}{dt}f+\frac{d}{dt}g \end{equation*}

Example 3

  • Let \(f,g:[0,1]\to\mathbb{R}\) be continuous, and \(\alpha\in\mathbb{R}\).
  • Then \((\alpha f+g):[0,1]\to\mathbb{R}\) is continuous, and
\begin{equation*} \int_0^1(\alpha f+g)(t)dt=\alpha\int_0^1f(t)dt+\int_0^1g(t)dt \end{equation*}

Example 4

  • Let \(x:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}^2\) (i.e. \(x(t)=\begin{pmatrix}x_1(t)\\x_2(t)\end{pmatrix})\).
  • Let \(A=\begin{pmatrix} a & b\\c & d\end{pmatrix}\) be a \(2\times 2\) matrix.

  • Then \((Ax):\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}^2\) is defined by

    \begin{equation*} (Ax)(t)=\begin{pmatrix} a & b\\c & d\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}x_1(t)\\x_2(t)\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}ax_1(t)+bx_2(t)\\cx_1(t)+dx_2(t)\end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}

Example 4

  • Let \(x,y:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}^2\), \(\alpha\in\mathbb{R}\), and \(A\) be a \(2\times 2\) matrix.

  • Then \((\alpha x+y):\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}^2\), and

    \begin{equation*} A(\alpha x+y)=\alpha Ax+Ay \end{equation*}

Pattern?

  • There are two patterns

Pattern 1: “Scale and Add”

  • \(v,w\in\mathbb{R}^n,\alpha\in\mathbb{R}\)

    \begin{equation*} \implies(\alpha v+w)\in\mathbb{R}^n \end{equation*}

  • \(f,g:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}\text{ differentiable }, \alpha\in\mathbb{R}\)

    \begin{equation*} \implies (\alpha f+g):\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}\text{ differentiable} \end{equation*}

  • \(f,g:[0,1]\to\mathbb{R}\text{ continuous },\alpha\in\mathbb{R}\)

    \begin{equation*} \implies(\alpha f+g):[0,1]\to\mathbb{R}\text{ continuous} \end{equation*}

  • \(x,y:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}^2,\alpha\in\mathbb{R}\)

    \begin{equation*} \implies (\alpha x+y):\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}^2 \end{equation*}

’Scale and Add’ Counterexample

Let \(f,g:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}\) be discontinuous at \(0\), and \(\alpha\in\mathbb{R}\). Does this guarantee that \((\alpha f+g):\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}\) is discontinuous at \(0\)?

’Scale and Add’ Counterexample

  • NO
  • Let \(f(x)=\begin{cases} 0 & x\le 0\\ 1 & x>0\end{cases}\), \(\alpha=1\) and \(g=-f\).
  • Then both \(f\) and \(g\) are discontinuous at \(0\)
  • But \((\alpha f+g)=(f-f)=0\) (the zero function) is continuous.

Pattern 2: “Commutes with Scale and Add”

  • \(A(\alpha v+w)=\alpha Av+Aw\)
  • \(\frac{d}{dt}(\alpha f+g)=\alpha\frac{d}{dt}f+\frac{d}{dt}g\)
  • \(\int_0^1(\alpha f+g)(t)dt=\alpha\int_0^1f(t)dt+\int_0^1g(t)dt\)
  • \(A(\alpha x+y)=\alpha Ax+Ay\)

’Commutes with’ Counterexample

  • If \(f,g:[0,1]\to\mathbb{R}\) are continuous and \(\alpha\in\mathbb{R}\), does that guarantee that

    \begin{equation*} \int_0^1(\alpha f(t)+g(t))^2dt=\alpha\int_0^1(f(t))^2dt+\int_0^1(g(t))^2dt \end{equation*}

’Commutes with’ Counterexample

  • NO

  • Let \(f(t)=g(t)=2\) and \(\alpha=1\). Then

    \begin{equation*} \int_0^1(\alpha f(t)+g(t))^2dt=\int_0^14^2dt=16 \end{equation*}

    But

    \begin{equation*} \alpha\int_0^1(f(t))^2dt+\int_0^1(g(t))^2dt=\int_0^12^2dt+\int_0^12^2dt=8 \end{equation*}

Vector Spaces

Intuitively a vector space is a set of objects, \(V\), called vectors such that

  • If \(\alpha\in\mathbb{R}\) and \(v,w\in V\), then \((\alpha v+w)\in V\).
    • ’+’ and ’\(\alpha v\)’ will either be explicitly defined, or defined by context

Examples of Vector Spaces

  • \(\mathbb{R}^n\) is a vector space.
  • \(\{f:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}|f\text{ is differentiable}\}\) is a vector space.
  • \(\{f:[0,1]\to\mathbb{R}|f\text{ is continuous}\}\) is a vector space.
  • \(\{x:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}^2\}\) is a vector space.
  • \(\{f:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}|f\text{ is discontinuous at }0\}\) is NOT a vector space.

Important Vector Spaces

Continuous Functions

  • Codomain is \(\mathbb{R}\)
    • \(C([a,b])=\{f:[a,b]\to\mathbb{R}|f\text{ is continuous}\}\)
    • \(C(\mathbb{R})=\{f:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}|f\text{ is continuous}\}\)
    • \(C(\mathbb{R}^n)=\{f:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}|f\text{ is continuous}\}\)
  • Specified codomain
    • \(C([a,b];\mathbb{R}^n)\) \(=\{f:[a,b]\to\mathbb{R}^n|f\text{ is continuous}\}\)
    • \(C(\mathbb{R};\mathbb{R}^n)=\{f:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}^n|f\text{ is continuous}\}\)
    • \(C(\mathbb{R}^n;\mathbb{R}^m)=\{f:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^m|f\text{ is continuous}\}\)

Continuously Differentiable Functions

  • Codomain is \(\mathbb{R}\)

    \begin{align*} C^1([a,b])&=\{f:[a,b]\to\mathbb{R}|\\ &f' \text{ exists and is continuous}\}\\ C^1(\mathbb{R})&=\{f:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}|\\ &f' \text{ exists and is continuous}\}\\ C^1(\mathbb{R}^n)&=\{f:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}|\\ &\text{all first-order partial derivatives}\\ &\text{exist and are continuous}\}\\ \end{align*}

Continuously Differentiable Functions

  • Specified codomain

    \begin{align*} C^1([a,b];\mathbb{R}^n)&=\{f:[a,b]\to\mathbb{R}^n|\\ &f' \text{ exists and is continuous}\}\\ C^1(\mathbb{R};\mathbb{R}^n)&=\{f:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}^n|\\ &f' \text{ exists and is continuous}\}\\ C^1(\mathbb{R}^n;\mathbb{R}^m)&=\{f:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^m|\\ &\text{all first-order partial derivatives}\\ &\text{exist and are continuous}\}\\ \end{align*}

Continuously Differentiable Functions

In the last slide, we meant:

  • For \(C^1(\mathbb{R};\mathbb{R}^n)\) \(\implies f(x)=(f_1(x),...,f_n(x))\) and \(f'(x)=(f_1'(x),...,f_n'(x))\).
  • For \(C^1(\mathbb{R}^n;\mathbb{R}^m)\) \(\implies f(x_1,....,x_n)=(f_1(x_1,...,x_n),f_2(x_1,...,x_n),...,f_m(x_1,...,x_m))\), and we are interested in \(\frac{\partial f_i}{\partial x_j}\), for \(i=1\cdots m\) and \(j=1...n\).

Spaces of Sequeneces

  • Sequence notation:
    • \(\{x_k\}_{k=1}^\infty=x_1,x_2,x_3,\cdots\)
    • \(\{x_k\}_{k=0}^\infty=x_0,x_1,x_2,\cdots\)
    • \(\{x_k\}_{k=-\infty}^\infty=\cdots ,x_{-2},x_{-1},x_0,x_1,x_2,\cdots\)
  • For example
    • \(\{k^2\}_{k=1}^\infty=1,4,9,16,\cdots\)

Linear Combinations of Sequences

  • If \(x=\{x_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\), \(y=\{y_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\), and \(\alpha\in\mathbb{R}\), then

    \begin{equation*} \alpha x+y=\{\alpha x_k+y_k\}_{k=1}^\infty \end{equation*}

  • For example, if \(x=\{k^2\}_{k=1}^\infty\), \(y=\{2k\}_{k=1}^\infty\), and \(\alpha=1\), then

    \begin{equation*} \alpha x+y=\{k^2+2k\}_{k=1}^\infty=3,8,15,24,\cdots \end{equation*}

Real Valued Sequences

  • Let
    • \(\mathbb{N}=\{1,2,3,\cdots\}\)
    • \(\mathbb{N}_0=\{0,1,2,\cdots\}\)
    • \(\mathbb{Z}=\{\cdots,-2,-1,0,1,2,\cdots\}\)
  • Vector spaces of sequences:
    • \(C(\mathbb{N})=\big\{\{x_k\}_{k=1}^\infty|x_k\in\mathbb{R}\text{ for all }k\in\mathbb{N}\big\}\)
    • \(C(\mathbb{N}_0)=\big\{\{x_k\}_{k=0}^\infty|x_k\in\mathbb{R}\text{ for all }k\in\mathbb{N}_0\big\}\)
    • \(C(\mathbb{Z})=\big\{\{x_k\}_{k=-\infty}^\infty|x_k\in\mathbb{R}\text{ for all }k\in\mathbb{Z}\big\}\)

Square Summable Sequences

  • \(\ell^2(\mathbb{N})=\{\{x_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\in C(\mathbb{N})|\sum\limits_{k=1}^\infty|x_k|^2<\infty\}\)
    • Pronounced “little ell two”.
    • Proof needed that this is a vector space.
  • Define \(\ell^2(\mathbb{N}_0)\) and \(\ell^2(\mathbb{Z})\) similarly.

Linear Transformations

Let \(V,W\) be vector spaces. A map \(T:V\to W\) is linear (or a linear transformation) if

  • for all \(v_1,v_2\in V\), and all \(\alpha\in\mathbb{R}\), then \(T(\alpha v_1+v_2)=\alpha T(v_1)+T(v_2)\).

Examples of Linear Transformations

For an \(m\times n\) matrix \(A\), define \(T_1:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^m\) by

\begin{equation*} T_1(v)=Av \end{equation*}

Then \(T_1\) is linear.

Examples of Linear Transformations

Define \(T_2:C^1(\mathbb{R})\to C(\mathbb{R})\) by

\begin{equation*} T_2(f)=\frac{d}{dt}f \end{equation*}

Then \(T_2\) is linear.

Examples of Linear Transformations

Define \(T_3:C([0,1])\to\mathbb{R}\) by

\begin{equation*} T_3(f)=\int_0^1f(t)dt \end{equation*}

Then \(T_3\) is linear.

Examples of Linear Transformations

For a \(2\times 2\) matrix \(A\), define \(T_4:C(\mathbb{R};\mathbb{R}^2)\to C(\mathbb{R};\mathbb{R}^2)\) by

\begin{equation*} T_4(x)=Ax \end{equation*}

Then \(T_4\) is linear.

Examples of Linear Transformations

Define \(T_5:C([0,1])\to \mathbb{R}\) by

\begin{equation*} T_5(f)=\int_0^1(f(t))^2dt \end{equation*}

Then \(T_5\) is not linear.

Examples of Linear Transformations

Define \(T_6:C([0,1])\to C(\mathbb{N})\) by

\begin{equation*} (T_6(f))_k=\sqrt{2}\int_0^1f(s)\sin(k\pi s)ds \end{equation*}

Then \(T_6\) is linear.

Theorem: (We might prove this at some point) Let \(f\in C([0,1])\), then \(T_6(f)\in\ell^2(\mathbb{N})\).

Examples of Linear Transformations

Define the right shift operator \(S:C(\mathbb{N})\to C(\mathbb{N})\) by

\begin{equation*} S(x_1,x_2,x_3,\cdots)=0,x_1,x_2,x_3,\cdots \end{equation*}

WARNING: \(S\) is one-to-one but not onto

Examples of Linear Transformations

Define the left shift operator \(S^*:C(\mathbb{N})\to C(\mathbb{N})\) by

\begin{equation*} S^*(x_1,x_2,x_3,\cdots)=x_2,x_3,x_4,\cdots \end{equation*}

WARNING: \(S^*\) is onto but not one-to-one
